Why USCIS Translation Requirements Are Strict

Under 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3), every foreign language document submitted to USCIS must be accompanied by a full English translation and a certification from the translator. The certification must state that the translator is competent to translate from Korean to English and that the translation is accurate and complete to the best of their knowledge.

USCIS does not accept partial translations, machine translations, or translations by the applicant or their immediate family members. Documents that do not meet these requirements are returned, delaying the application and sometimes causing it to be denied entirely if deadlines are missed.

The types of Korean documents most commonly submitted to USCIS include birth certificates, marriage certificates, divorce decrees, police records, military service records, academic transcripts, and financial documents. Each of these has specific formatting expectations, and Korean government documents often use official seals, stamps, and administrative language that require a translator who understands both the document format and the legal terminology involved.

What a USCIS-Compliant Translation Must Include

A compliant translation is more than just an accurate rendering of the Korean text into English. It must meet several specific requirements.

The translation must be complete. Every element of the original document must be translated, including headers, footers, stamps, seals, signatures, and any printed text that appears anywhere on the document. Partial translations are not accepted.

The certification statement must be signed by the translator and must include their name, address, and the date of certification. It must explicitly state that they are competent in both Korean and English and that the translation is accurate and complete to the best of their knowledge.

The layout of the translation should mirror the original document as closely as possible. USCIS officers compare the translated document to the original, and a translation that reorganizes or reformats the content can raise questions about its accuracy.

Common Mistakes That Lead to USCIS Rejections

Applicants often underestimate how specific USCIS requirements are until an application is returned. These are the mistakes that most commonly cause problems with Korean document translations.

Using machine translation tools like Google Translate is a common error. Machine translation cannot produce the certification statement USCIS requires, and machine translations of complex Korean administrative language are often inaccurate in ways that are not obvious to someone who does not read Korean.

Asking a bilingual friend or family member to translate is another frequent mistake. Even if that person is fluent in both languages, USCIS specifically prohibits translations by the applicant themselves and may question translations done by immediate family members. The translation must come from a neutral, qualified party.

Omitting any part of the original document is also a common source of rejection. Korean government documents frequently include information printed in small text around the margins, official seals with text inscribed on them, and stamps with dates and reference numbers. All of this must be translated.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does USCIS accept translations done by family members?

No. USCIS requires that translations be completed by someone who is competent in both the source and target languages and who can certify the accuracy of the translation. The applicant themselves cannot translate their own documents. Translations by immediate family members, while not explicitly prohibited in all cases, are strongly discouraged and may be questioned by USCIS officers.

How long does a certified Korean translation take?

Most standard documents such as birth certificates and marriage certificates take one to three business days. Longer documents such as court records, academic transcripts, or employment history files may take three to five business days. Can I use the same translated document for multiple USCIS submissions?

Generally yes. A properly certified translation of a document can be submitted as part of multiple applications. However, if the original document has been updated or reissued, a new translation will be needed. Some applications also require translations that are recent, so check the specific requirements for each application type.

What Korean documents are most commonly required for green card applications?

The most frequently required documents for Korean applicants seeking permanent residency include birth certificates for the applicant and any accompanying family members, marriage certificates, police clearance certificates from Korean authorities, military service records for male applicants, and financial documents if sponsorship is involved.
